WWII’s Secret Weapon: The Hershey Bar Designed to Taste Like a "Boiled Potato"

When you unwrap a Hershey’s chocolate bar today, you expect a sweet, creamy treat that practically melts in your mouth. But if you were a soldier jumping into Normandy or trekking through the Pacific theater during World War II, handing you a Hershey bar wasn't a reward. It was a survival tactic.

During the war, the U.S. military teamed up with the iconic Pennsylvania chocolate company to create something entirely counterintuitive: a chocolate bar specifically engineered to be tough, heat-resistant, and completely unappetizing.

This was the Field Ration D—better known as the D Ration. It played a critical, if universally despised, role in the Allied war effort, and the story behind it is a masterclass in weird military engineering.

A Very Unusual Request

The story starts in April 1937, a few years before the U.S. officially entered the war. Captain Paul Logan of the U.S. Army Quartermaster Corps walked into the Hershey Chocolate Corporation to pitch an idea to the company’s president, William Murrie, and chief chemist, Sam Hinkle.

With global tensions escalating, the Army knew they needed a lightweight, high-energy survival ration. They needed something that paratroopers, infantry, and downed pilots could carry in their pockets to stave off starvation if they got cut off from supply lines.

Logan had four absolute, non-negotiable requirements for this new chocolate bar:

  1. It had to weigh exactly four ounces.
  2. It had to pack a massive caloric punch (at least 600 calories per bar).
  3. It had to withstand high temperatures without melting—up to 120°F.
  4. It had to taste terrible.

Well, maybe not terrible, but Logan explicitly requested that the bar taste "about as good as a boiled potato."

"If it is too good," Logan explained, "the soldier will eat it as a snack instead of saving it for an emergency."

The military brass knew human nature. If they handed a hungry 18-year-old a delicious piece of milk chocolate, he was going to eat it on an empty stomach. The ration had to be palatable enough to keep a starving man alive, but miserable enough that he wouldn't touch it unless his life depended on it.

Breaking the Chocolate Machines

Creating a high-calorie bar was the easy part for Hershey’s chief chemist, Sam Hinkle. The real nightmare was engineering a chocolate bar that wouldn’t melt in the blistering heat of the North African desert, while simultaneously dulling the natural sweetness of the cocoa.

To pull this off, Hinkle's team created a dense recipe utilizing chocolate liquor, sugar, skim milk powder, cocoa fat, and a dash of artificial vanilla flavoring. But the secret ingredient—the one that really gave the bar its structural integrity and "boiled potato" vibe—was oat flour.

This concoction was a logistical nightmare for Hershey's factory. Normal chocolate flows down the production line as a warm, silky liquid, easily poured into molds. The D Ration recipe created a thick, heavy, crumbly paste that wouldn't flow at any temperature. Hershey literally had to invent and build custom machines just to mechanically smash this heavy dough into the four-ounce molds.

"Hitler's Secret Weapon"

By 1939, the Army was thrilled with the final product. By the height of the war, Hershey was churning out an incredible 24 million D Ration bars every single week.

But while the generals loved the logistics, the soldiers absolutely hated the chocolate.

Because of the heavy oat flour and the distinct lack of cocoa butter, the D Ration was notoriously rock-hard. If a soldier tried to take a bite out of it like a normal candy bar, he was risking a chipped tooth.

Troops had to get creative just to eat the things. Many used their issued combat knives to shave thin slivers off the block, letting the dry flakes slowly dissolve in their mouths. Others would crush the bar with a rock or the butt of their rifle, throw the chunks into a canteen cup with boiling water, and choke it down as a thick, gritty hot cocoa.

The bars were so universally disliked that soldiers gave them a series of dark nicknames, most notably "Hitler's Secret Weapon." This was partly due to the taste, but also because eating the rich, dense bar too quickly on an empty stomach often caused intense gastrointestinal distress. Men suffering from dysentery found them physically impossible to digest. When they weren't being eaten in sheer desperation, D Rations were frequently discarded or used as barter with unsuspecting locals.

A Bitter Legacy

Despite being the most hated candy bar in American history, the D Ration was an undeniable success. It did exactly what Captain Logan asked it to do: it survived the harshest environments on the planet and kept thousands of stranded men from starving to death.

In 1943, after years of complaints from the front lines, the Army finally asked Hershey to develop the "Tropical Bar." It was slightly softer and tasted a bit better, though it still wouldn't win any culinary awards. For their massive contribution to the war effort, the Hershey Chocolate Corporation was awarded the Army-Navy ‘E’ Award for Excellence in Production five separate times.

The D Ration remains a brilliant piece of utilitarian history. It wasn't pretty, it definitely wasn't tasty, and it wasn't meant to be enjoyed. But when the chips were down, that boiled-potato-flavored block of chocolate did exactly what it was supposed to do: it brought soldiers home.
