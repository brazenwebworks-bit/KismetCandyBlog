The Origins of Chocolate

Long before it was a sweet treat wrapped in foil, chocolate was a sacred beverage, a currency, and a medicine. Its journey from the rainforests of Mesoamerica to the candy aisles of the world is a rich tapestry of history.

The Food of the Gods

The story begins with the Olmecs, and later the Mayans and Aztecs, who cultivated the cacao tree. They believed cacao was a gift from the gods. Unlike today's hot chocolate, their version, xocolatl, was bitter, frothy, and often spiced with chili peppers.

"Money literally grew on trees: cacao beans were used as currency in the Aztec empire."

When Spanish explorers brought cacao to Europe, they added sugar and honey to counteract the bitterness. This sweetened version became a hit among the aristocracy, sparking a chocolate craze that swept across the continent.

The Industrial Revolution

It wasn't until the 19th century that chocolate became accessible to the masses. Inventions like the cocoa press allowed for the separation of cocoa butter and cocoa powder, paving the way for the creation of solid chocolate bars. Pioneers like Fry, Cadbury, and Nestle revolutionized the industry.

Today, we are seeing a return to the roots of chocolate, with a focus on single-origin beans and artisanal production. But every time you snap a piece of dark chocolate, you are taking a bite of history that spans thousands of years.
